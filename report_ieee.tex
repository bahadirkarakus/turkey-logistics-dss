\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a web-based Decision-Support System (DSS)
for optimising freight distribution across a Turkey-wide logistics network
composed of five production centres and eight regional warehouses.
The system formulates the distribution problem as a linear programming (LP)
transportation model and solves it with the CBC simplex solver via PuLP.
Beyond deterministic optimisation, the platform integrates four analytical
modules: sensitivity analysis via dual variables and reduced costs;
Monte Carlo simulation of demand uncertainty with truncated-normal sampling;
a multi-objective Pareto frontier balancing cost against travel time; and
a scenario manager supporting rapid what-if comparisons.
A Streamlit web dashboard delivers interactive maps (Folium/AntPath),
Sankey flow diagrams, heatmaps, and downloadable PDF reports.
Results demonstrate that routing 1{,}410 units across 8--10 active routes
achieves a minimum total transport cost of approximately
\textlira 118{,}000--168{,}000 depending on the operating scenario,
with shadow-price analysis identifying Antalya and Gaziantep demand nodes
as the binding constraints most sensitive to cost changes.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
transportation problem, linear programming, decision support system,
Monte Carlo simulation, Pareto frontier, Streamlit, logistics optimisation
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Efficient freight distribution is a critical challenge for manufacturers
operating national supply chains.
Turkey's geography---spanning Thrace, the Aegean, central Anatolia, the
Black Sea coast, and the Mediterranean---creates substantial variation in
transport distances, road tolls, and seasonal demand patterns.
Practitioners often resort to manual allocation or heuristic spreadsheet
tools that cannot guarantee optimality or quantify the cost of uncertainty.

This work describes a Decision-Support System (DSS) that addresses these
shortcomings by embedding a classical LP transportation model inside an
interactive web application.
The platform allows analysts to:
\begin{itemize}
  \item select predefined scenarios (Normal Season, Summer Season,
        Fuel Increase, Winter Season) or customise supply and demand
        parameters in real time;
  \item visualise optimal shipment routes on an animated interactive map;
  \item run sensitivity analysis to identify which constraints are
        most valuable to relax;
  \item simulate the cost distribution under stochastic demand via
        Monte Carlo methods;
  \item trace the Pareto frontier between cost minimisation and
        travel-time minimisation;
  \item save and compare multiple solved scenarios side-by-side;
  \item export a structured PDF report for stakeholders.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:problem} states the mathematical problem formulation.
Section~\ref{sec:cost} describes the cost estimation model.
Section~\ref{sec:solution} outlines the solution approach.
Section~\ref{sec:architecture} summarises the system architecture.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents deterministic optimisation results.
Sections~\ref{sec:scenario}--\ref{sec:pareto} cover the analytical modules.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Network Structure}

The logistics network consists of a set of production centres
$I = \{$\.Istanbul, Ankara, \.Izmir, Bursa, Adana$\}$ and
a set of warehouses
$J = \{$Konya, Kayseri, Trabzon, Gaziantep, Antalya,
Samsun, Eski\c{s}ehir, Diyarbak\i r$\}$.
Each production centre $i \in I$ has a fixed supply capacity $s_i$ (units),
and each warehouse $j \in J$ has a demand $d_j$ (units).
The network is \emph{unbalanced} with total supply exceeding total demand
($\sum_i s_i = 2{,}650 > \sum_j d_j = 1{,}410$ units),
so surplus capacity is absorbed via slack variables.

\subsection{Decision Variables}

$$x_{ij} \geq 0 \quad \forall\, i \in I,\; j \in J$$

where $x_{ij}$ is the number of units shipped from production centre $i$
to warehouse $j$.

\subsection{Objective Function}

Minimise the total transportation cost:
\begin{equation}
  Z = \sum_{i \in I}\sum_{j \in J} c_{ij}\, x_{ij}
  \label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}

where $c_{ij}$ (TL/unit) is the unit transport cost on route $(i,j)$.

\subsection{Constraints}

\textit{Supply constraints} (capacity cannot be exceeded):
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{j \in J} x_{ij} \leq s_i \quad \forall\, i \in I
  \label{eq:supply}
\end{equation}

\textit{Demand constraints} (all demand must be fully satisfied):
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{i \in I} x_{ij} \geq d_j \quad \forall\, j \in J
  \label{eq:demand}
\end{equation}

The model contains $|I| \cdot |J| = 40$ decision variables and
$|I| + |J| = 13$ functional constraints.

%=======================================================================
\section{Cost Estimation Model}
\label{sec:cost}

Unit transport cost $c_{ij}$ is derived from a per-trip cost model
divided by truck capacity $q$:

\begin{equation}
  c_{ij} = \frac{
    d_{ij}\!\cdot\!\frac{L}{100}\!\cdot\! p_f
    \;+\; \frac{d_{ij}}{v}\!\cdot\! w
    \;+\; \tau_{ij}
    \;+\; f
  }{q}
  \label{eq:cost}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $d_{ij}$ --- road distance (km), sourced from Google Maps
        approximations or optionally fetched via the OSRM routing API
        \cite{osrm};
  \item $L/100 = 30$ --- fuel consumption (L/100 km) for a heavy goods vehicle;
  \item $p_f = 40$ TL/L --- diesel price (adjustable via live input);
  \item $v = 80$ km/h --- average driving speed;
  \item $w = 150$ TL/h --- driver wage;
  \item $\tau_{ij}$ --- HGS/OGS highway toll (TL per truck), route-specific;
  \item $f = 200$ TL --- fixed loading/unloading fee per trip;
  \item $q = 25$ --- truck capacity in distribution units.
\end{itemize}

The fuel multiplier $\mu_f \geq 0$ scales the entire cost matrix to
model fuel price shocks:
$c_{ij}^{(\mu)} = c_{ij} \cdot \mu_f$.

%=======================================================================
\section{Solution Approach}
\label{sec:solution}

The model is implemented in Python using PuLP \cite{pulp},
an open-source LP modelling library.
The CBC (Coin-or Branch and Cut) simplex solver \cite{cbc}
is invoked with output suppressed.
For a balanced/unbalanced transportation problem of this size
(40 variables, 13 constraints), the simplex method typically converges
in under 50 pivots, producing solutions in milliseconds.

After solving, the system extracts:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the optimal shipment plan $\{x_{ij}^* > 0\}$;
  \item slack values $\sigma_i = s_i - \sum_j x_{ij}^*$ per source;
  \item demand fulfilment $\delta_j = \sum_i x_{ij}^*$ per warehouse;
  \item dual variables (shadow prices) $\pi_i$, $\lambda_j$
        for sensitivity analysis.
\end{itemize}

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

The DSS is structured as a five-module Python package.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_architecture.png}
  \caption{System architecture: data layer, solver, analytics, visualisation,
           and Streamlit front-end.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\textbf{data.py} holds the network topology, distance matrix, toll table,
cost parameters, scenario definitions, and the OSRM API client.

\textbf{model.py} wraps the PuLP LP formulation and returns a structured
result dictionary including the PuLP problem object for post-solve access
to dual variables.

\textbf{analytics.py} implements sensitivity analysis (dual variables and
reduced costs), Monte Carlo demand simulation, and multi-objective
Pareto-frontier enumeration.

\textbf{visualization.py} builds all Plotly and Folium figures:
the animated AntPath route map, Sankey flow diagram, cost breakdown bar
chart, scenario comparison chart, and cost heatmap.

\textbf{app.py} is the Streamlit entry point.
It manages session state, wires the sidebar controls to the solver and
analytics modules, and renders the eight-tab dashboard.

The application ships with a Dockerfile for containerised deployment on
any platform supporting Docker \cite{docker}.

\section{Scenario Analysis}
\label{sec:scenario}

Four predefined scenarios capture common operating conditions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Normal Season}: base demand, $\mu_f = 1.0$.
  \item \textbf{Summer Season}: Antalya $+40\%$, Trabzon $+20\%$,
        Samsun $+15\%$ demand; fuel unchanged.
  \item \textbf{Fuel Increase (+20\%)}: $\mu_f = 1.20$; demand unchanged.
  \item \textbf{Winter Season}: inland cities up ($+10$--$20\%$),
        coastal resorts down ($-20$--$30\%$); $\mu_f = 1.05$.
\end{itemize}

Users select a scenario, press \textit{Run Optimisation}, then
\textit{Save Result}, and repeat for each scenario before pressing
\textit{Compare Saved}.
The comparison view (Fig.~\ref{fig:scenario}) shows a grouped bar chart of
total cost with a secondary axis for percentage change relative to the first
saved scenario.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_scenario.png}
  \caption{Scenario comparison: total cost and percentage deviation
           from the base (Normal Season) result.}
  \label{fig:scenario}
\end{figure}

The Fuel Increase scenario yields the largest cost increase
($\approx +20\%$, proportional to the multiplier) because the cost model
is linear in $p_f$.
The Summer Season scenario raises total demand to $\approx 1{,}558$ units
and shifts the cost curve due to longer average hauls to coastal resorts.

\section{Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:sensitivity}

After solving, dual variables are extracted from PuLP via
\texttt{constraint.pi} (shadow prices) and \texttt{variable.dj} (reduced costs).

The \textbf{shadow price} $\pi_i$ of supply constraint $i$ equals the
decrease in total cost achievable by relaxing that supply limit by one unit.
The shadow price $\lambda_j$ of demand constraint $j$ equals the increase
in total cost required to serve one additional unit of demand at warehouse $j$.

The \textbf{reduced cost} $r_{ij}$ of a non-basic variable $x_{ij}$ equals
the amount by which the unit cost $c_{ij}$ must decrease before route
$(i,j)$ becomes part of the optimal basis.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_sensitivity.png}
  \caption{Shadow prices for supply nodes (top) and demand nodes (bottom),
           and reduced costs for inactive routes (right).}
  \label{fig:sensitivity}
\end{figure}

In the Normal Season base case, Antalya and Gaziantep show the highest
demand shadow prices, indicating that a unit of additional demand at
these locations would increase total cost most.
Conversely, \.Istanbul has a near-zero supply shadow price, confirming
that its large idle capacity is non-binding.

\section{Monte Carlo Simulation}
\label{sec:montecarlo}

To quantify cost risk under demand uncertainty, a Monte Carlo simulation
draws $N$ independent realisations of the demand vector and solves the LP
for each.
Warehouse demand is modelled as a truncated normal distribution:

\begin{equation}
  \tilde{d}_j \sim \mathcal{TN}\!\left(d_j,\;(\mathrm{CV}\cdot d_j)^2,\;
  \tfrac{d_j}{2},\; 2d_j\right)
  \label{eq:mc}
\end{equation}

where $\mathrm{CV}$ (coefficient of variation) controls dispersion.
The default settings are $N = 300$ and $\mathrm{CV} = 0.15$,
producing a $\pm 15\%$ standard deviation.

Key statistics extracted from the simulation:
mean cost $\bar{Z}$, standard deviation $\sigma_Z$,
5th and 95th percentile costs (risk range), and route reliability
(fraction of iterations in which each route carries positive flow).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_montecarlo.png}
  \caption{Monte Carlo cost histogram (300 iterations, CV = 0.15)
           with mean marked. Lower panel: route reliability by percentage
           of iterations active.}
  \label{fig:montecarlo}
\end{figure}

Results show that the 90\% cost interval spans roughly
$\pm 8$--$12\%$ around the deterministic optimum,
and that core routes (e.g., Adana$\to$Gaziantep, Ankara$\to$Konya)
maintain $>95\%$ reliability, while peripheral routes fluctuate.

\section{Multi-Objective Pareto Frontier}
\label{sec:pareto}

A decision maker may wish to reduce delivery time even at higher monetary
cost.
To explore this trade-off, a weighted-sum scalarisation sweeps
$\alpha \in [0, 1]$ in $K = 12$ equally spaced steps, solving:

\begin{equation}
  \min\quad \alpha\, \hat{Z}_\text{cost} + (1-\alpha)\, \hat{Z}_\text{time}
  \label{eq:pareto}
\end{equation}

where normalised objectives are:

$$\hat{Z}_\text{cost} = \frac{\sum_{ij} c_{ij} x_{ij}}{500{,}000},
\quad
\hat{Z}_\text{time} = \frac{\sum_{ij} (d_{ij}/v)\, x_{ij}}{2{,}000}$$

Each solve yields a cost--time pair; non-dominated pairs form the
Pareto frontier.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_pareto.png}
  \caption{Pareto frontier: total cost (TL) vs.\ total weighted travel time
           (h$\cdot$unit). Each marker corresponds to a different
           $\alpha$ weight.}
  \label{fig:pareto}
\end{figure}

The frontier reveals that significant time savings (e.g., $-15\%$)
are achievable with only a modest cost premium ($\approx +5\%$) at
$\alpha \approx 0.7$, while pushing toward $\alpha = 0$ (pure time
minimisation) incurs disproportionately large cost increases.

%=======================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented the Turkey Logistics DSS, an end-to-end transportation
optimisation platform combining classical LP with four decision-support
analytics modules.
The system demonstrates that:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item LP-based transportation models can be embedded in interactive
        web applications without sacrificing solver performance;
  \item scenario managers and shadow-price dashboards substantially reduce
        the cognitive overhead of what-if analysis;
  \item Monte Carlo simulation closes the gap between deterministic optima
        and real-world demand uncertainty;
  \item weighted-sum Pareto enumeration provides intuitive, actionable
        cost-versus-time trade-off information.
\end{enumerate}

Future work includes integrating live fuel price feeds from the Turkish
Energy Market Regulatory Authority (EPDK), incorporating stochastic road
conditions via updated OSRM queries, and extending the model to a
multi-period planning horizon.
